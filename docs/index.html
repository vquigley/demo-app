What goes wrong


The learning problem: AI doesn't retain anything between sessions. You fix the same misunderstandings repeatedly.
The confidence problem: AI confidently writes broken code and tells you it's great. You review everything line by line. Slower than writing it yourself.
The context limit: Large codebases overwhelm the AI. You're constantly re-explaining your own system.
The "faster to do it myself" trap: Sometimes true. And that thought kills the learning curve.

Sound familiar? Good. Here's what I changed.

Inoculate before instruction. Get the fears on the table so they can absorb the solutions without anxiety. Transition: "Okay, now that I've thoroughly scared you. [Pause, smile]. Here's the good news."

Shift 1: Set the agent up for success

Give it the context you'd give a new engineer picking up a ticket.

Give it the context you know

Don't assume the AI will figure something out. It's smart, but it isn't a mind reader. Share what you know about the problem, the codebase, the constraints.

Point at existing patterns

"We did something like this in [file]. Look at how we approached it. Look at old commits."

These are the highest-leverage changes. Give context solves the "AI doesn't learn" problem. Existing patterns leverage your codebase knowledge. Transition: "And two more that compound on these..."

Shift 2: Explore problems,
don't give orders

Stop telling AI what to build. Start telling it what to solve.

Frame problems, not tasks

"Build me a function that validates the daily limit" → prescriptive in, prescriptive out. Usually wrong.
"Users are seeing budget alerts with zero usage. Here's the relevant code. What could cause this?" → the AI investigates, asks questions, lays out approaches with tradeoffs.

The spec-first pattern

Session 1: Think through the problem with AI. Write a short spec.
Session 2: Fresh session implements from the spec.

"I stopped telling the AI what to build and started telling it what to solve." This is the fundamental mindset shift. Most people use AI like a command line. Type instruction, get output. But the best results come from collaboration, not commands. Spec-first is the demo. Transition: "And two more that compound on these..."

Push back when it's wrong

AI will confidently go the wrong direction. Say "that's not right, here's why." It course-corrects fast. Your domain knowledge is the steering wheel.

Use Plan Mode

Claude's Plan mode is much quicker and cheaper to validate the Agent is on the right direction than jumping straight into code.

Shift 1 is about what you prepare. Shift 2 is about how you collaborate.

"It's like rubber duck debugging, except the duck talks back." These techniques work together. Plan mode surfaces the right questions. Pushing back keeps the AI honest. And the conversation itself clarifies your own thinking. Transition: "Let me show you both shifts in action."
